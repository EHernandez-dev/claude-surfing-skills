## Overview

Mundaka is a **rivermouth** break over a **sand** bottom (behaves like a reef despite the sand) at the mouth of the Oka River / Urdaibai estuary, [Bizkaia, Basque Country, Spain](https://www.google.com/maps/search/?api=1&query=43.4095,-2.6995). It breaks predominantly **left** (a shorter right also exists). It suits **advanced/experienced** surfers when firing, and is best from **October to February/March**; summer (like now) is mostly flat. Consistency: **fickle and tide-windowed**, roughly a 2-hour window around the right tide, with pristine conditions rarely holding more than two days. The break is located at 43.4095, -2.6995 ([Google Maps](https://www.google.com/maps/search/?api=1&query=43.4095,-2.6995) | [Windy waves](https://www.windy.com/-Waves-waves?waves,43.4095,-2.6995,10)).

When it's on, Mundaka produces long, hollow, fast lefts of 150-300 m (500 m+ on exceptional days), a former Billabong Pro world tour venue (1999-2009). It is currently, and for the whole coming week, flat: none of the forecast swells reach the sandbar's minimum size or period.

**Works on:** NW groundswell, starts around 3-5 ft (1.0-1.5 m) at 12-14 s+, optimal 6-10 ft faces, holds 12 ft+. Offshore wind from S/SW (also SE). Low to mid tide, best on the push just after the low turn. *(Sources agree on low-to-mid; [Wannasurf](https://www.wannasurf.com/spot/Europe/Spain/Pais_Vasco/Mundaka/) notes both rising and falling tides can work, with low-past-the-turn avoiding the strongest undertow.)*

**Faces:** N (350° true) out to sea. Offshore wind blows from the S/SW.

## The Wave

Mundaka is a rivermouth sandbar that, despite its sand bottom, breaks with the mechanics of a reef: a shallow, well-defined bank at the mouth of the Oka River holds its shape session after session rather than shifting like a typical beach break. On a working swell, [Wannasurf](https://www.wannasurf.com/spot/Europe/Spain/Pais_Vasco/Mundaka/) and [SurferToday](https://www.surfertoday.com/surfing/mundaka-the-surfing-capital-of-spain) describe a steep, sucking takeoff at the peak near the harbor wall that pitches into a long, fast, hollow left, sectioning down the sandbar for 150-300 m and occasionally further on exceptional days. A shorter right peels off the same peak but is rarely the draw.

Size and tide change its character significantly: it starts working around 3-5 ft, is at its best (most makeable, still very hollow) at 6-10 ft, and holds shape up to 12 ft+ where it becomes a serious, heavy proposition. On tide, sources broadly agree low-to-mid is best, with the wave losing shape as the tide pushes toward high; [Wannasurf](https://www.wannasurf.com/spot/Europe/Spain/Pais_Vasco/Mundaka/) notes both the rising and falling tide can work, but the period just past the low turn is often preferred because the undertow is less severe than right at the high-tide turn.

None of this is relevant tomorrow: with 1.3-1.6 ft of swell at 7.4-7.5 s forecast, the wave has nowhere near the size or period to engage the sandbar at all.

## Hazards

**Rip currents:** A strong tidal current and rip run along the estuary channel, strongest on the outgoing tide. Locals use this channel current as a conveyor back out to the lineup on a working day; if you're caught in it unintentionally, do not fight it straight in, angle across it toward the harbor or beach side. [Wannasurf](https://www.wannasurf.com/spot/Europe/Spain/Pais_Vasco/Mundaka/) notes the undertow is strongest around the high-tide turn.

**Rocks / reef:** When the wave is breaking, the standard entry is a jump off the harbor wall into deep water; exit is picking a path through rocks on the south side of the harbor, or via the stairs below the church (rocks beneath the stairs too). [Laidatxu beach](https://www.google.com/maps/search/?api=1&query=Playa+Laidatxu+Mundaka) is the easy, safe in-and-out alternative with no rock hazard.

**Wave hazards:** When breaking, expect a steep, sucking, pitching takeoff onto a shallow sandbar, very fast and hollow faces, and potentially long hold-downs; some locals wear helmets on big days. None of this is applicable this week, the spot is flat.

**Marine life:** [Wannasurf](https://www.wannasurf.com/spot/Europe/Spain/Pais_Vasco/Mundaka/) and [SurferToday](https://www.surfertoday.com/surfing/mundaka-the-surfing-capital-of-spain) list sharks as a nominal hazard for the area, but no documented recent incidents were found. Urchins and jellyfish are not specifically documented for this spot.

**Crowds & localism:** Mundaka carries a heavy localism reputation; [The Inertia](https://www.theinertia.com/) lists it among Europe's most localized waves, with a narrow takeoff zone (room for roughly 20 surfers) and a tight, skilled Basque crew from Bilbao, Bermeo, and Gernika. This is reputation and intimidation rather than a documented incident record. Etiquette if you do surf it on a working day: do not paddle straight to the peak, wait your turn down the line, be patient and humble. Irrelevant on a flat July day like tomorrow, but very relevant if you return in the autumn/winter season.

**Skill-level match:** As a first-timer at this spot, when Mundaka is actually breaking it is advanced-only: do not paddle out at the rivermouth peak on a firing day as a first session, regardless of your general ability level elsewhere. On tomorrow's flat day, the physical danger at Mundaka itself is low, but so is the reward, since there's no wave. The estuary beaches (Laida, Laga, Laidatxu) are the right call for an actual surf tomorrow.
