## Overview

Ocean Beach is a **beach break** over **sand** that breaks **both ways** (shifting lefts and rights across constantly moving sandbars). It suits **advanced and expert** surfers and is best in **fall and winter** (January is the standout month). Consistency: **very consistent**. The break is located at 37.759, -122.513 ([Google Maps](https://www.google.com/maps/search/?api=1&query=37.759,-122.513) | [Windy waves](https://www.windy.com/-Waves-waves?waves,37.759,-122.513,10)).

A heavy, powerful, cold-water beach break stretching roughly 3.5 miles along the western edge of the city. It is punchy and fast when a clean groundswell lines up with morning offshores, but it closes out and turns brutal above head-high, and the tidal flow out of the Golden Gate shifts the sandbars hour to hour. This week is classic summer Ocean Beach: foggy, mostly short-period WNW windswell, and onshore afternoon sea breeze, with only glassy dawn windows worth chasing.

**Works on:** W (through SW to NW) groundswell, roughly 3-15+ ft at 10s+, E offshore wind, workable at most tide stages but leaning mid-to-low. *(Synthesized from spot guides; the tide claim is disputed and is noted in The Wave and Information Gaps.)*

**Faces:** W (265° true) out to sea. Offshore wind blows from the E.

## The Wave

Ocean Beach is a long, fully exposed, sand-bottom beach break that catches almost every swell that reaches the California coast. [surf-forecast.com](https://www.surf-forecast.com/breaks/Ocean-Beach) rates it very consistent and describes lefts and rights "so fast it is hard to slow down," stacking into walls and barrels on bigger swells. It works best on a **W swell with E offshore wind**, ideally clean long-period groundswell rather than the wind-driven slop it gets in summer.

Because the beach runs for miles, the takeoff is not one peak but a shifting series of them. [San Francisco Surfing](https://sanfranciscosurfing.com/ocean-beach) names the Sloat Boulevard area and Kelly's Cove at the north end as reference peaks, but stresses that "sandbars shift constantly, so conditions vary dramatically from week to week." The local move is to pick a peak and stay near it rather than drifting with the current. [surf-forecast.com](https://www.surf-forecast.com/breaks/Ocean-Beach) notes that all the water moving in and out of the Golden Gate sweeps up and down the beach with enough force to dislocate the swell and rearrange the sandbars hour to hour, so the same bank can be good and then unsurfable within a tide.

Size and tide change the character sharply. It is fun and rippable at head-high and under, but [Wannasurf](https://www.wannasurf.com/spot/North_America/USA/California/SF_Marin/ocean_beach/) warns it is only for those in exceptional shape above 8-10 ft, and it will hold 15 ft-plus, which is where the early Mavericks crew built their big-wave paddle fitness. [San Francisco Surfing](https://sanfranciscosurfing.com/ocean-beach) notes low tide becomes shallow and closeout-prone and calls mid-tide optimal.

**Sources disagree on the ideal tide.** [surf-forecast.com](https://www.surf-forecast.com/breaks/Ocean-Beach) says there is good surf at all stages, while [San Francisco Surfing](https://sanfranciscosurfing.com/ocean-beach) and the general reputation of the spot favor mid-to-low, with low tide risking closeouts on some banks. Resolve it on the beach for the specific sandbar you are looking at.

## Hazards

**Rip currents:** This is the defining hazard of Ocean Beach. [Wannasurf](https://www.wannasurf.com/spot/North_America/USA/California/SF_Marin/ocean_beach/) calls the rips "a local legend" and very strong, and notes rangers hand out an information sheet before you go in. A UC Berkeley coastal oceanographer has [warned](https://news.berkeley.edu/2017/05/24/expert-on-rip-currents-warns-of-dangers-at-san-franciscos-ocean-beach/) that lives are lost here every year when people are knocked off their feet and swept out hundreds of feet in minutes. Currents can pull you rapidly north or south as well as offshore. The [Golden Gate National Parks Conservancy](https://www.parksconservancy.org/park-e-ventures-article/hidden-dangers-ocean-beach) documents the same. If caught, do not fight it: paddle parallel to the beach until you are out of the flow, then in. Surfers use the low-current dawn window to paddle out, and the currents strengthen on this week's big tide swings.

**Rocks / reef:** Predominantly clean sand, but [surf-forecast.com](https://www.surf-forecast.com/breaks/Ocean-Beach) explicitly says "beware of rocks," and the far north end near Kelly's Cove and the Cliff House sits closer to rocky shoreline. The main body of the beach is sand-bottom.

**Wave hazards:** Heavy, punchy shore break and powerful sets. The wave closes out and becomes dangerous above roughly 8-10 ft ([Wannasurf](https://www.wannasurf.com/spot/North_America/USA/California/SF_Marin/ocean_beach/)), and low tide can go shallow and close out ([San Francisco Surfing](https://sanfranciscosurfing.com/ocean-beach)). The paddle-out through successive lines of whitewater is notoriously exhausting, which is exactly why big-wave surfers train here.

**Marine life:** Ocean Beach lies inside the "Red Triangle," the stretch of Northern California coast with the highest concentration of recorded great white shark encounters, driven by large seal and sea lion populations ([Red Triangle, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_Triangle_(Pacific_Ocean))). [surf-forecast.com](https://www.surf-forecast.com/breaks/Ocean-Beach) lists sharks among the hazards. In perspective: attacks are rare (an individual surfer's odds are cited at roughly 1 in 17 million), peak white-shark activity is the fall "Sharktober" window of September through November, and rip currents and cold water are a far greater real-world risk than sharks ([Shark Stewards](https://sharkstewards.org/sharktober-avoiding-a-great-white-shark-attack-in-the-red-triangle/)). Documented attacks at Ocean Beach itself are uncommon.

**Crowds & localism:** [surf-forecast.com](https://www.surf-forecast.com/breaks/Ocean-Beach) lists "locals" among the hazards, but the miles of shifting peaks spread surfers out, so Ocean Beach is generally less territorial than a defined point or reef. It is a core, committed crowd rather than a heavily localized one. [Wannasurf](https://www.wannasurf.com/spot/North_America/USA/California/SF_Marin/ocean_beach/) frames it as a spot for the adventurous and dedicated surfer.

**Skill-level match:** Advanced and expert. Guides put the minimum at solid intermediate-to-advanced, and the spot is honestly no place for beginners: cold water, exhausting paddle-outs, strong rips, and powerful surf. At head-high and under on a clean bank it is a rewarding advanced beach break; above 8-10 ft it is for the exceptionally fit only. If you are learning, use a nearby alternative (see below).

## Access & Logistics

- **Parking:** Free. There are free lots at the north and south ends along the Great Highway, plus abundant free street parking along the Great Highway and La Playa and on the perpendicular streets. ([Inside Guide to San Francisco](https://www.inside-guide-to-san-francisco-tourism.com/ocean-beach-san-francisco.html))
- **Entry / exit:** Cross the Great Highway to the sand. Pick a peak (Sloat, Kelly's Cove, and the banks in between are common references) and paddle out at a low-current window, typically early morning; stay near your peak rather than drifting with the current.
- **Facilities:** Few noted at the beach itself; [Wannasurf](https://www.wannasurf.com/spot/North_America/USA/California/SF_Marin/ocean_beach/) lists no facilities. Restrooms and food exist up near the Beach Chalet and Cliff House ends. Muni N-Judah and L-Taraval lines reach the area.
- **Fees:** None noted for parking or beach access.
